History Of Graffiti

From Cave Walls to Subway Cars

01. Ancient Intent

Graffiti isn't a modern "problem"—it's a human instinct. Citizens in Ancient Rome scratched political slogans and declarations of love into city walls. In Pompeii, the ash preserved everything from tavern jokes to poetic insults. Even Viking mercenaries left their mark in the Hagia Sophia, carving "Halvdan was here" into the marble.

02. Philadelphia & NYC (1960s-70s)

The modern era exploded in Philly with Cornbread. By the early 70s, the movement shifted to NYC. A courier named TAKI 183 became a legend simply by writing his name everywhere. It wasn't about "art" yet; it was about getting up—establishing presence in a crowded city.

03. The Subway Era (1980s)

The "tag" evolved into the "throw-up" and eventually the Masterpiece. NYC subways became moving galleries. Artists like Dondi, Phase 2, and Lady Pink pioneered styles like "Wildstyle"—complex, interlocking lettering designed to be unreadable to the "toys" (unskilled beginners).

04. Post-Graffiti & Street Art

By the late 80s, the "War on Graffiti" pushed artists off trains and into the streets. This birthed Street Art. While traditional graffiti focuses on the name, street art (think Banksy or Shepard Fairey) often uses icons and stencils to convey social messages.
